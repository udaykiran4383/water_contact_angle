Motivation

Wettability is fundamental; measuring it is expensive

  • The contact angle θ is the primary observable of wetting — coatings, surface treatments, adhesion, superhydrophobicity.
  • Commercial goniometers (Krüss, Biolin, ramé-hart, DataPhysics) reach ±0.1° — at ≈ ₹15 lakh per instrument.
  • Key insight: they achieve this with modest 1–5 MP cameras. Their accuracy lives in optics and software, not the sensor.
  • A smartphone sensor exceeds those cameras — so a careful optical bench + rigorous on-device software should approach lab-grade results.

Background

The physics of the measurement

Young's equation — force balance at the three-phase line:

γSV − γSL = γLV cos θ

Young–Laplace (Bashforth–Adams form) — the equilibrium drop profile, arc length s scaled by apex radius b:

dφ/ds = 2 − βz − sin φ / x,   β = Δρ g b²/γ

  • β → 0: drop is a spherical cap; gravity flattens larger drops.
  • Exact geometric bound: θ ≥ 2·atan(h/a) — used later as a physical gate on degenerate fits.
  • At small β the Young–Laplace fit is unidentifiable (angle valid, surface tension not) — flagged, not over-claimed.

The measurement image: a back-lit drop silhouette on its substrate (synthetic 125° cap — angle known exactly, used for validation).

Young 1805 · Bashforth & Adams 1883 · Hoorfar & Neumann 20063

Background

Where the error actually comes from

Published error analysis of goniometry (Vuckovac et al., Soft Matter 2019):

  • ±1 px of baseline error → ≈0.5–1° of angle error below 150°, growing to ≈8° as θ→180°.
  • Error scales inversely with drop size in pixels.
  • Seven experts analysing the same image spread over ~10°.

Design rules adopted:

  • Determine the baseline algorithmically, to sub-pixel precision.
  • Fill the frame with the drop; warn when it is small.
  • Propagate baseline sensitivity into every reported uncertainty.

Capture

Metrology-aware capture: discipline before pixels

  • Live attitude badge from the accelerometer — roll (baseline leveling; green ≤0.5°) and pitch (optical axis; ISO 19403-2 recommends 0–4° downward so contact points and reflection are visible). Shutter ring turns green only in tolerance.
  • Tap-to-lock focus — autofocus hunting changes magnification mid-session.
  • −1 EV exposure bias + lock — bright field kept just below clipping; the sub-pixel edge survives.
  • Zoom from a distance — poor-man's telecentricity; less perspective error than shooting close.

Post-capture quality control (automatic)

  • Highlight clipping > 2% saturated pixels → blooming → retake prompt
  • Silhouette contrast (p95 − p5) too low → retake prompt

Why it matters: every item maps 1-to-1 onto an entry in the error budget of slide 4 — geometry and exposure faults are cheaper to prevent at capture than to correct in software.

Pipeline · 1

Silhouette extraction with sub-pixel edges

Why not Canny/Sobel? The dark drop merges into the dark substrate (no edge exists between them); the interior refraction window creates strong false edges.

Approach — use the one reliable contrast (bright field vs dark object):

  • Otsu threshold → dark objects; connected component above the baseline isolates the drop.
  • Row-scan of outermost pixels → immune to interior artefacts; continues to the per-column baseline so stage tilt cannot amputate the downhill contact.
  • Needle-in-drop detected as a sustained narrow apex run and trimmed to the shoulder.

Sub-pixel localisation

For an anti-aliased edge, the true boundary lies exactly at the 50%-coverage intensity crossing between local background and object levels. Every horizontal edge point and every baseline point is refined to this crossing — integer edges cap accuracy at ±0.5 px, worth ≈0.5° on small drops since dθ ≈ σbaseline/a.

All fits then operate in the baseline-aligned frame (true rotation), with left and right angles computed independently and tilt-corrected — the practice of every reference instrument.

Pipeline · 3

Four independent angle-extraction methods

Circle Algebraic LS + geometric R². Exact at β→0. Gate fixed so hydrophilic caps (centre below baseline — correct geometry) are accepted.

Ellipse Halir–Flusser stable direct conic fit; correct eigenvector via full 3×3 characteristic cubic; analytic tangents at the baseline.

Tangent Polynomial fit in a frame rotated to the local tangent (weighted PCA): the profile is single-valued at any θ — removes the −5° steep-flank fold bias of image-frame fitting at 145°.

Young–Laplace (ADSA) the physical reference method:

  • RK4 integration of Bashforth–Adams from the apex (local error ≈10⁻⁹)
  • Orthogonal residuals; multi-start Nelder–Mead over Bond seeds; robust trimming
  • Contact-zone exclusion: the optically distorted ≈2 px band above the contact is excluded; θ read by extrapolating the fitted profile to the baseline (ADSA-NA practice). A proportional band tested worse — decisions made by measurement.
  • Degeneracy gates: reject θ < 0.6 × 2 atan(h/a) (flat-solution artefact); flag β < 0.05 as circle-degenerate.

A near-perfect ADSA fit anchors the ensemble; otherwise methods are combined with quality weights, Tukey outlier moderation and leave-one-out consistency.

dθ/dh = 1/(r·sin θ) = 1/a  →  reproduces the published 0.5°/px plateau and the θ→180° blow-up

ISO-style quality flags on the result card

  • θ ≥ 150° — baseline sensitivity grows steeply
  • θ ≤ 20° — tangent placement ambiguous
  • stage tilt > 2° — level the stage
  • |θL − θR| > 4° — non-axisymmetric drop or baseline error
  • drop < 100 px half-width — error ∝ 1/size

The result card also reports per-method angles, acceptance/rejection reasons, and the baseline detector used — the measurement is auditable, not a black box.

Case study · The problem

A hazard we identified on our own rig: where is the baseline?

Our working sketch: Case A — shadow not considered, a “possible baseline” appears above the true one; Case B — shadow considered, drop + shadow form wedges at the contacts.

The mechanism. Under the drop's overhang the back-light is blocked: the drop, its shadow and its reflection merge into one dark region. Two candidate baselines appear — the true contact plane, and the shadow/reflection boundary below it. At ≈0.5–1° per pixel (slide 4), picking the wrong one costs tens of degrees.

Methods we applied to solve it:

  • Specular mirror-symmetry: treat the reflection as signal — the baseline is the sub-pixel symmetry axis of the drop + reflection blob (waist test, θ>90°).
  • Glossy step detection: plateau-midpoint crossing on object-free flank columns finds the surface even when it is brighter than the threshold.
  • Junction-corner refinement: the corner of each flank's outermost-edge trace (slope-sign flip) marks the contact on both sides → baseline and tilt.
  • Arbitration + proof: strict precedence rules between detectors, then synthetic wedge images with exactly known θ locked in as regression tests.
Same signal family as Krüss ADVANCE, the Dropometer and DropSnake — implemented and verified on-device16

The contact-shadow hazard — identified, solved, proven

Honest boundaries

Known limitations — characterised, not hidden

  • Shadow-skirt regime (external glycerol images): a soft halo widens monotonically into the substrate edge — no wedge apex, waist or corner exists for any trace-geometry detector. Costs 12–20° there. Fix path known: mirrored-model image-energy fit (Stalder) or learned segmentation.
  • Small drops: error ∝ 1/pixel-size — flagged below 100 px half-width; the remedy is optical (zoom / move closer).
  • θ → 180°: baseline sensitivity 1/(r sinθ) diverges; results ≥150° carry a warning, consistent with published advice to prefer force-based methods there.

Future work

  • Mirrored-model baseline for the skirt regime
  • Needle-side independent fitting (Albert 2019, ≤0.6°)
  • Video capture: per-frame angles over a stability plateau (mean ± SD)
  • In-frame scale calibration → volume, physical Bond number, surface tension in the identifiable regime
  • Small on-device segmentation model as a fallback for messy field images
Each limitation is measured on named images and documented with its literature fix path18
